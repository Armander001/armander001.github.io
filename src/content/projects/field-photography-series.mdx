## The question

A dataset can tell you a transect was 30 meters long and took 40 minutes. It can't tell you what the visibility was like, how the current was pulling at the tape measure, or what it looks like when three divers are quietly doing the same repetitive task for the fortieth time this season. This series asks whether photographing the *work* of reef research — not just its subjects — is itself a form of documentation worth keeping.

## Approach

The rule for this series is simple and mostly about restraint: shoot what's actually happening during a survey, not a staged version of it. That means as much time behind the camera pointed at tape measures, slates, and dive buddies as pointed at coral. Available natural light is preferred over strobes for most frames, partly for logistics and partly because it keeps the color and mood closer to what a diver's eye actually sees at depth, rather than the saturated, strobe-lit look typical of reef photography.

Editing leans toward sequences rather than single "hero" images — a set of six or eight frames that reads as a scene (setting up a transect, a coral bleaching survey in progress, the boat ride home) does more to communicate field research than any single striking photo could.
