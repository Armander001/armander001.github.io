## The question

A field team can survey a few kilometers of reef in a season. A Sentinel-2 tile covers 100 km on a side and arrives every five days, free. The question is whether that open archive can be turned into habitat maps trustworthy enough to base decisions on — and how to make the map's uncertainty as visible as its colors.

## Approach

Shallow water punishes shortcuts. Before any classification, each scene passes through a correction chain: cloud and cloud-shadow masking, atmospheric correction, then sun-glint removal using the near-infrared signal over optically deep water. Rather than classifying single scenes, the workflow builds multi-temporal composites — a per-pixel median across the clearest scenes of a season — which suppresses residual glint, waves, and turbidity events that no single-scene method fully removes.

Training data comes from georeferenced field surveys at [Site name] : photo-quadrat points labeled by benthic class (coral, algae, seagrass, rubble, sand) and split site-wise, not randomly, so validation actually tests generalization to unseen reef rather than memorization of neighboring pixels. A random forest does the classification; nothing fancier survived contact with the accuracy assessment.

The step most maps skip is the one this project treats as the product: a full error matrix from a stratified, independently sampled validation set, reported per class. Users see not just "seagrass here" but how often "seagrass" is right — and where the map is guessing.

## Outcome

The workflow now covers [N] km of reef shelf at 10 m resolution, refreshed as new clear-sky Sentinel-2 scenes arrive, with a published per-class accuracy figure attached to every map release rather than a bare classification image. That error matrix has become the thing collaborators ask for first — a map people can calibrate their trust in is more useful than a marginally prettier one they can't.
